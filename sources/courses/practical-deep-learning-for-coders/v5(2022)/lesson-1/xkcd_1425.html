What was impossible back then is possible now!

The first lecture of the Practical Deep Learning for Coders course kicked-off by the xkcd comic, numbered 1425. Jeremy shared the comic to give a glimpse how computer tasks that were impossible a few years ago can be done in under 2 minutes!

I did some further research on the comic, and found some interesting things. This blog posts shows that.

Background

In the year 1966, Marvin Minsky assigned a couple of his undergraduate students at MIT to spend the summer programming a computer to use a camera to identify objects in a scene. He suggested “linking a camera to a computer and getting it to describe what it saw”.

The Plan

A plan was drafted by Seymour Papert.

The goal was to allocate that full summer and complete this task They really hoped they would be done with it by the end of that summer. Against their anticipation, not only the task was finished, but people were still working on this for the next half a century.

The Comic

xkcd published the following comic in 2014 to depict the situation back then.

In 2014, it was nearly impossible to create a code that can check if an image is of a bird or not! So impossible that it became the idea of a joke.Everybody at that time knew that it was impossible!

AZ: What happened with AlexNet that got released in 2012?

The Jump

Now in 2022, we can pull it off for free in under 2 minutes with very high accuracy Is It A Bird?. It is amazing how much things have changed, although it took us more than 5 years!

Comic Breakdown

If you are new to, xkcd comics, the character on the left is Cueball and on the right is Ponytail.

Cueball is asking Ponytail to create an app that can determine if a given picture is:

  1. taken in a national park, and
  2. a picture of a bird.

The first task is generally harder for a human, but easier for an app that has access to:

  1. location information and
  2. a geographic information system (GIS).

That is why  Ponytail says “Sure, easy GIS lookup. Give me a few hours.”!

To tackle the first task of determining whether a person is in a national park, she plans to get the person’s location using his/her mobile, and then cross checked this location with a geographic information system (GIS), which can get the coordinates.

The second task is easy for a human, but harder for an app (at least it was untill 2014) due to many reasons.

All of this is an example of Moravec’s Paradox, proposed in the 1980s.

Post Reactions On The Comic

A month after the comic was released, Flickr responded with a prototype online tool(offline as of now!) which attempted to do what the comic described.

They used an Automated Tagging Software.

According to them, the bird solution “took us less than 5 years to build, though it’s definitely a hard problem, and we’ve still got room for improvement”.
